Mapping NYC Evictions: Which Tenants to Support?

Evictions are a leading cause of homelessness, an issue both the Mayor of New York City and his administration have made a priority. Designed to reduce and mitigate both homelessness and ongoing impacts of gentrification, urban policies can be enhanced by spatial analysis of historical eviction locations and rates. This spatial analysis utilizes Marshal Evictions in 2017 across New York City, mapping both disaggregated incidents and offering summarizations to neighborhoods and community districts.

Exploring Tax Map Changes in New York City, 2009-2018

Utlizing tax maps from the NYC Department of Finance, this project ascertained individual map changes 2009 -2018; visualized those changes and aggregated the results to community districts thorughout the 5 boroughs. Tax map changes have occurred in almost every community district with significant variance across the city. Further, this project highlighted large scale regions with high volume changes, utilizing zoning to explore those changes in detail. This project offers insight into the where the tax map has changed across time, and highlights select segments of the city that have experience the most flux over time.

Tree Cover Loss in Peruvian Forests: Is the National Forestry and Climate Change Strategy - designed to protect forest cover - an effective policy measure ?

The aim of this project is to compare the deforestation in the Peruvian Amazon during years 2016 and 2017, consistent with the implementation of the Monitoring System -GEOBOSQUES - Forest and Climate Change Strategy (Ministry of Environment). The spaital comparisons are conducted at the regional level - (San Martin, Madre de Dios, Huanuco and Ucayali) - and account for deforestation before and following the Strategy implementation. The resulting project cartography includes one national map and three regional maps. The spaital analysis includes quantification and summary of forest loss temporally and spatially across the national and regional study areas.

Spatial Mismatch in South Africa: Mapping Cape Town, Durban, and Johannesburg

This project aims to investigate spatial mismatch in South African cities, using Cape Town, Durban (eThekwini), and Johannesburg as case studies. As a result of apartheid planning, most South African cities experience spatial segregation, with jobs being concentrated in urban cores, and vulnerable populations, including poor, black South Africans, living in the peripheries of these areas. Because of this fact, many South Africans must travel long distances to access employment, meaning that it is more difficult for them to find and maintain jobs. Using the spatial mismatch theory suggests that, because of the spatial layout of South African cities, unemployment rates will be higher the further away from the central economic cores of the city, and might disproportionately affect black and poor South Africans. In order to analyze the level of spatial mismatch in South African cities, job concentrations and proximities were mapped to to demographic features, demonstrating that unemployment and the distance are spatially related, with race and income an influencing variable.

Spatial Analysis of the 2018 Midterm Election in Texas

This spatial analysis seeks to ascertain voting trends of within Texas spanning the 2016 Presidential Election to the 2018 Midterm Election. For the past 30 years, Texas has historically been a RED state. In 2016, Texas voted for Donald Trump (R) over Hillary Clinton (D) with almost a 10% spread. In 2018, during the midterm elections, Texas saw the party spread shrink to just a 2.6% win by Ted Cruz (R) over Beto O’Rourke (D). Specifically, this study aims to illustrate shifts in party affiliation voter turnout by county in Texas in the 2018 Midterm Elections compared to the 2016 Presidential Election. Further, bot urban and rural county designations coupled with population densities are incorporated to denote spatial intersection and possible correlation.

500 Cities and the Individual Mandate

The Affordable Care Act (ACA) has had a number of iterations; each of which was adopted based on the political exigencies of the legislative and political environment. In 2006 one of the first contemporary iterations of The Affordable Care Act (ACA) was colloquially known as “RomneyCare.” By 2010 “RomneyCare” had become “ObamaCare. Many Americans don’t realize that ObamaCare is the same as the ACA. In this geospatial analysis I situate the reality of the often amorphous ACA in physical space by using federally collected data on health outcomes. This spatial analysis seeks to address three primary questions:

  1. How drastically did the individual mandate impact healthcare enrollment?
  2. Did states that accepted expanded medicaid see measurable differences in health outcomes?
  3. What trends can be expected entering 2019?
