The Impact of Violence on Food Insecurity. Case study: The 2011 Famine in Somalia

This project seeks to problematize the spatial relationship between food insecurity and violence through the case study of 2011 Famine in Somalia. It is widely assumed that the major cause of famine that struck East Africa in June 2011 was the catastrophic drought. Although the entire Somalia was affected by drought, the famine struck the most in the southern parts of the country. I ask then, what are the other socio-economic conditions that might have contributed to the outburst of famine in June 2011 ? By following the approach of Alex de Waal (2018) on the predominantly political nature of famines and violent character of Somalia political regime, this research aims to visualize whether violence might have been an important factor in the outburst of 2011 famine in Somalia. By using a GIS analysis, I hope to show that the problem of food availability in Somalia was closely linked not only to catastrophic drought, but also to the growing outbursts of violence in the years leading up to the 2011 famine. By tracking and relating to each other the precipitation anomalies for 2011 relative to a historical preceding decade (2001-2010), the livelihood zones of Somalia for 2011, and the changes in density of violent events for 2001-2005 (time of relative peace) and 2006-2011 (years leading to famine) I want to show that the issues of food security are closely related to violent patterns. Although various famine-monitoring databases closely observe regions associated with food insecurity, rarely other than climate and weather-related variables are taken into consideration. In most of the cases, the famine early warning system tracks droughts, changes in food security, and livelihood zones. Other social and cultural factors — such as patterns of violence, infrastructure, and character of households — are rarely (if ever) monitored and included in famine analysis and relief struggles. Through this research, I want to demonstrate that without addressing the problem of structural violent patterns countries, such as Somalia, will be at further risk of food insecurity.

Sea Level Rise and Storm Surges: Mapping the Impacts on New York City

The goal of this project was to quantify and visualize potential impact geographies intersecting with a 6-foot Sea Level Rise or a 6-foot storm surge on New York City coastline. While most may not see a 6-foot Sea Level Rise in our lifetimes, we will on the other hand see a 6-foot storm surge. Hurricane Sandy brought storm surges ranging from 6 to 9 feet to the New York City region. The goal of this analysis encompasses the entire NYC region to find the most vulnerable locations. Project criteria included: Urban vs. Green Landcover, Percent Submerged, Percent Urban Area Submerged, and Areas with High Population Density. Four regions were identified as significant impact.
